## Summary

Fuzz seed `20260732` exposed a duplicate receiver `not-ready` publish after an explicit leave. The fix is to treat published `not-ready` receiver-readiness states as control-plane tombstones: cache clears and suppressed non-routable refreshes may defer ready publications, but they must not erase already-published tombstone evidence.

## Problem

- `just reliability-fuzz-local-overnight 20260703 100` failed at seed `20260732`.
- Artifact: `/tmp/turbo-scenario-fuzz/20260602-230828-90371f35/seed-20260732`.
- Scenario execution and engine trace replay were green, but strict diagnostics failed with `receiver.readiness_duplicate_publish_without_recovery_boundary`.
- The concrete duplicate was two equivalent `receiver-not-ready` publications for the same contact/channel/control-plane epoch: first from `channel-refresh`, then from `ptt-sync`.

## Design formulation

- Owner: app control-plane reducer/effect executor.
- Invariant: once a receiver `not-ready` tombstone has been published for a contact, equivalent later `not-ready` syncs are idempotent until a recovery boundary changes the published readiness state.
- State-machine rule: suppression may represent unsent local state while the peer is non-routable, but it must not downgrade an equivalent already-published tombstone to an unpublished suppressed state.
- This shape keeps published control-plane boundary evidence monotonic and prevents a later sync source from re-emitting the same tombstone.

## What changed

- `ControlPlaneCoordinator` now preserves deferred and `not-ready` receiver-readiness states when clearing a specific contact cache entry.
- Non-routable receiver-readiness sync no longer overwrites an equivalent already-published readiness state with a suppressed state.
- Added reducer regressions for preserving published `not-ready` across cache clear and non-routable suppressed refreshes.
- Fixed a test compile blocker in `ConnectionTests` where a `TurboBeginTransmitResponse` override closure was missing `return`.

## What worked

- Engine trace replay stayed green, which ruled out the engine as the source owner.
- The failing fuzz replay stayed red after only preserving cache clears, proving there was a second path.
- The narrow reducer regression captured the actual source: a non-routable channel refresh during leave-in-flight downgraded published `not-ready` to suppressed `not-ready`; a later `ptt-sync` republished it.
- After the reducer fix, strict diagnostics found no current or historical invariant violations and no duplicate readiness publishes for the failing seed.

## What not to repeat

- Do not treat receiver-readiness cache clears as a generic dictionary delete. Ready publications are cacheable freshness; `not-ready` publications are tombstone evidence.
- Do not let a suppressed state replace an equivalent published state merely because the peer is currently non-routable. That turns an idempotent boundary into an accidental publish gap.
